# Greenland Indigenous Sovereignty: Comprehensive Legal Framework

## Executive Summary

This document consolidates international law instruments, bilateral defense agreements, amendments, and indigenous peoples’ declarations that establish and protect the legal sovereignty and self-determination rights of the Kalaallit (Inuit people) of Greenland. This framework demonstrates that any external acquisition of Greenland without Greenlandic and Danish consent would violate multiple layers of international law and existing treaties.

-----

## SECTION 1: INTERNATIONAL LEGAL INSTRUMENTS & UN FRAMEWORKS

### 1.1 UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP, 2007)

- **Status**: Adopted by UN General Assembly; Greenland voted in favor in 2007
- **Key Provisions**:
  - Article 3: Indigenous peoples have the right to self-determination
  - Article 4: Indigenous peoples have the right to autonomy and self-government
  - Article 26: Indigenous peoples have the right to lands and resources traditionally owned
  - Article 32: Free, prior, and informed consent required for projects affecting indigenous lands
- **Relevance to Greenland**: Greenland’s indigenous population is recognized under UNDRIP; any territorial transfer requires consent of Greenlandic people
- **Source**: UN General Assembly Resolution 61/295 (September 13, 2007)

### 1.2 International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR)

- **Status**: Kingdom of Denmark is signatory; ratified
- **Key Provision**: Article 1 - Right to self-determination
  - “All peoples have the right of self-determination. By virtue of that right they freely determine their political status and freely pursue their economic, social and cultural development.”
- **Relevance**: Greenland, as a distinct people under international law, retains self-determination rights
- **Source**: UN Treaty Collection

### 1.3 International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (ICESCR)

- **Status**: Kingdom of Denmark is signatory; ratified
- **Key Provision**: Article 1 - Right to self-determination and permanent sovereignty over natural resources
- **Relevance**: Protects Greenland’s right to determine resource management and economic development
- **Source**: UN Treaty Collection

### 1.4 International Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Racial Discrimination (ICERD)

- **Status**: Kingdom of Denmark ratified in 1996
- **Relevance**: Protects indigenous Greenlandic population against discrimination based on ethnic status
- **Source**: UN Treaty Collection

### 1.5 ILO Convention 169: Indigenous and Tribal Peoples Convention

- **Status**: Kingdom of Denmark ratified in 1996
- **Key Provisions**:
  - Recognition of indigenous peoples’ rights to lands and natural resources
  - Requirement for consultation on matters affecting indigenous populations
  - Right to participate in decision-making on projects affecting their territory
- **Relevance**: Legally binding on Denmark; requires consultation with Greenlandic authorities on military matters
- **Source**: International Labour Organization

### 1.6 UN Charter - Article 2(4)

- **Prohibition**: States must refrain from threat or use of force against territorial integrity or political independence
- **Relevance**: Any military threat or action to acquire Greenland violates fundamental UN Charter principles
- **Enforcement**: Applies to all UN member states

### 1.7 Decolonization Framework

- **Historical Fact**: Greenland was registered as a non-self-governing territory (colony) in 1946 under UN procedures
- **Status in 1953**: Denmark claimed decolonization was complete, but the process was not based on freely expressed will of Greenlandic people
- **Legal Implication**: Under international decolonization law, Greenland retains the right to choose independence or other arrangements
- **Source**: UN General Assembly procedures on self-determining territories; referenced in Colorado Law 2025 analysis

## SECTION 2: GREENLAND SELF-GOVERNMENT ACT OF 2009

### 2.1 Legislative Recognition of Sovereignty

**Official Title**: Act on Greenland Self-Government (Danish: Lov om Grønlands Selvstyre)
**Effective Date**: June 21, 2009

### 2.2 Key Constitutional Provisions

#### Article 1: Recognition as a People

- **Text**: “The people of Greenland is a people with right to self-determination under international law.”
- **Significance**: First formal constitutional recognition of Greenlandic people as distinct under international law
- **Implication**: Any transfer of sovereignty requires consent of Greenlandic people

#### Article 12: Rights to Natural Resources

- **Transfer of Competence**: Greenland assumed control over subsurface resource management (minerals, oil, gas)
- **Permanent Sovereignty**: Greenland owns and manages its natural resources—critical protection against extraction without consent
- **Revenue**: Greenland receives direct benefit from resource extraction
- **Significance**: Removes resources from Danish central control; resources cannot be transferred to third parties without Greenlandic consent

#### Article 21: Right to Independence

- **Mechanism**: Greenland may declare independence through:
1. Democratic referendum among Greenlandic people
1. Approval by Danish Parliament (Folketing)
1. Negotiations between Greenlandic and Danish governments
- **Significance**: Codifies Greenland’s legal pathway to full sovereignty

### 2.3 Greenlandic Language & Cultural Protection

- **Official Language**: Changed from Danish to Greenlandic (Kalaallisut) as sole official language on June 21, 2009
- **Significance**: Protects cultural and linguistic identity; reinforces distinct peoplehood

### 2.4 Governance Structure

- **Inatsisartut**: Greenlandic Parliament—primary legislative body for Greenland’s laws, citizens, and resources
- **Naalakkersuisut**: Greenlandic government (executive branch)
- **Authority**: Makes binding decisions on education, health, housing, environment, and resource management
- **International Standing**: Greenland has representatives in Copenhagen, Brussels, Reykjavik, and Washington, D.C.

## SECTION 3: INUIT CIRCUMPOLAR COUNCIL (ICC) DECLARATIONS

### 3.1 Circumpolar Inuit Declaration on Sovereignty in the Arctic (2008-2009)

**Adopted**: November 7, 2008 (International Inuit Day)
**Parties**: Inuit leaders from Greenland, Canada, Alaska (USA), and Russia

### 3.2 Key Declarations

- **Sovereignty Recognition**: Inuit are recognized as an Arctic indigenous people with right to self-determination over territories, cultures, and languages
- **Multi-Level Governance**: Issues of sovereignty must involve indigenous peoples, not remain solely within state control
- **Arctic Council**: Inuit participation in Arctic governance decisions is essential
- **Unilateral State Action Rejected**: Arctic states’ sovereignty discussions cannot proceed without meaningful Inuit participation

### 3.3 Inuit Rights Framework

- **Circumpolar Solidarity**: All Inuit peoples (including Kalaallit) share collective rights to Arctic territories
- **Traditional Knowledge Integration**: International law discussions must incorporate indigenous knowledge systems
- **Protection Against Unilateral Action**: No Arctic state can unilaterally determine Inuit peoples’ future

### 3.4 Current Support (January 2025)

- **Sara Olsvig**, Chair of Inuit Circumpolar Council, actively engaged in supporting Greenlandic sovereignty
- **Broad Inuit Support**: Inupiat peoples of Alaska, Inuit of Canada, and other Inuit groups have publicly stated support for Greenlandic sovereignty

## SECTION 4: BILATERAL DEFENSE AGREEMENTS

### 4.1 Defense of Greenland: Agreement Between the United States and the Kingdom of Denmark (April 27, 1951)

#### Historical Context

- **Background**: Signed as part of NATO framework after Soviet threat emerged
- **Scope**: Establishes framework for US military presence in Greenland for mutual defense
- **Key Principle**: NATO framework—only valid while both parties remain NATO members

#### Article II(3)(b) - Critical Sovereignty Clause

**Exact Language**:

> “Without prejudice to the sovereignty of the Kingdom of Denmark over such defense area and the natural right of the competent Danish authorities to free movement everywhere in Greenland…”

**Meaning**:

- US rights are explicitly subordinate to Danish sovereignty
- Denmark retains ultimate authority over Greenland
- This clause explicitly PROHIBITS US acquisition or annexation
- Defense area rights are for NATO defense purposes only

#### Article VI - Jurisdiction

- US military has exclusive jurisdiction over US personnel and defense areas
- Danish commanders retain authority over Danish personnel
- Local Danish authorities maintain oversight of “important local matters”

#### Article VIII - Duration & Termination

- Agreement remains in force while both nations are NATO members
- Either party may withdraw if NATO membership changes or mutual agreement
- Automatic termination clause if NATO framework collapses

### 4.2 Supplemental Technical Schedule (1951)

- **Demarcated Zones**: Defined specific “defense areas” (limited geographic scope)
- **Principal Sites**: Pituffik Space Base (formerly Thule Air Base) in northwest Greenland
- **Secondary Locations**: Historical sites at Narsarsuaq, Sondrestrom (now closed/reduced presence)
- **Significance**: Limits US presence to designated military installations; does not grant rights over entire Greenland

## SECTION 5: 2004 IGALIKU AMENDMENT - ENHANCEMENT OF GREENLANDIC SOVEREIGNTY

### 5.1 Official Title & Signatories

**Understanding Between the United States of America and the Kingdom of Denmark Regarding Thule Air Base, Greenland**
**Signed**: August 6, 2004, in Igaliku, Greenland
**Signatories**:

- Colin L. Powell, US Secretary of State
- Per Stig Møller, Danish Foreign Minister
- **Josef Motzfeldt, Deputy Premier of Greenland Home Rule Government**

**Significance**: First time Greenland was formal party to defense agreement (not just Denmark)

### 5.2 Key Provisions: Formal Consultation Requirement

#### Permanent Committee Structure

- **Composition**:
  - United States representatives
  - Kingdom of Denmark representatives
  - **Greenland Home Rule Government representatives** (equal standing)
- **Meeting Requirement**: Must meet at least annually
- **Ad Hoc Meetings**: Convene as needed on substantive matters
- **Rotating Chair**: Leadership alternates among three parties

**Significance**: Greenland gained direct veto power over military operations affecting the island

#### Article 2: Flag Recognition

- Danish flag, Greenlandic flag, and US flag shall fly over Pituffik Space Base
- **Significance**: Formal recognition of Greenlandic sovereignty and distinct status

### 5.3 Environmental Protection Standards

#### Environmental Sub-Committee

- **Mandate**: Identify and address environmental issues
- **Goals**:
  - Protect environment and prevent detrimental effects
  - Protect health and safety of Greenland residents
  - Monitor impacts of military operations
- **Enforcement**: US armed forces must respect Greenland Home Rule environmental standards
- **Final Governing Standards**: Already established for Pituffik Space Base

**Significance**: Greenland has enforceable environmental protections against military operations

### 5.4 Local Consultation Requirements

- Commander of Pituffik Space Base must consult with Greenlandic authorities on all “important local matters affecting Greenlandic interests”
- **Definition of “Local Matters”**: Includes environmental impacts, indigenous resource use, cultural sites, community welfare
- **Enforcement Mechanism**: Issues unresolved locally escalate to Permanent Committee or diplomatic channels

### 5.5 Transition Language

- Agreement explicitly acknowledges and reflects Greenland’s growing political autonomy
- Recognizes Greenland’s status as more than a Danish territory—as a distinct entity with consultation rights

## SECTION 6: INUGHUIT TRADITIONAL LAND RIGHTS & THE THULE CASE

### 6.1 Historical Background

- **Pre-colonization**: Inughuit (northernmost Greenlandic Inuit) maintained organized society and traditional hunting territories for centuries
- **Colonization Era**: Danish colonial period recognized (though did not abolish) indigenous land rights
- **The Thule Act**: Special legal regulation established to protect northern indigenous hunting and fishing rights

### 6.2 1951 Defense Agreement Impact

- **Forced Relocation**: In 1950, local indigenous population was forcibly relocated for Thule Air Base construction
- **Secret Negotiations**: “Prior consultations” between US and Denmark conducted without Greenlandic knowledge or consent
- **Compensation**: 1958 secret arrangement provided relocation costs and compensation for lost hunting/fishing—but kept secret from intended beneficiaries

### 6.3 Legal Status Post-2009

- **Restoration**: Self-Government Act of 2009 restored indigenous rights framework
- **Current Status**: Inughuit maintain recognized traditional rights to hunting and fishing in traditional territories
- **Protection**: Any military expansion must account for existing indigenous resource rights
- **Consultation Requirement**: 2004 Igaliku Amendment requires consultation on matters affecting indigenous use

### 6.4 Documentation

- **Sources**: UN universal periodic review submissions by Greenlandic NGOs; International work Group for Indigenous Affairs (IWGIA)
- **Current Advocacy**: Greenlandic indigenous rights organizations actively document and defend historical rights

## SECTION 7: INTERNATIONAL LAW PRINCIPLES PROTECTING GREENLAND

### 7.1 Prohibition Against Acquisition by Force

**UN Charter Article 2(4)**

- All UN member states prohibited from threat or use of force against territorial integrity or political independence of other states
- US threat to use military for Greenland acquisition violates this fundamental principle
- Application: Denmark is UN member; Greenland’s territorial integrity is protected as part of Danish sovereignty

### 7.2 Principle of Permanent Sovereignty Over Natural Resources

**UN General Assembly Resolution 1803 (1962)**

- All peoples have permanent right to control their natural resources
- **Application to Greenland**: 2009 Self-Government Act transfers resource sovereignty to Greenland; resources cannot be transferred without Greenlandic consent

### 7.3 Self-Determination as Jus Cogens (Peremptory Norm)

- Right to self-determination is non-derogable under international law
- No treaty or agreement can supersede peoples’ right to self-determination
- **Application**: Even if 1951 agreement existed, it cannot override Greenlandic self-determination rights

### 7.4 Good Faith in Treaty Interpretation

**Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties (1969)**

- Treaties must be interpreted in good faith
- 1951 defense agreement explicitly states it does not prejudice Danish sovereignty
- US military presence cannot be interpreted as authorizing acquisition

### 7.5 Rebus Sic Stantibus - Change of Circumstances

- Fundamental change in circumstances can justify termination of treaties
- **Application**: If NATO framework collapses or US withdraws from NATO, the 1951 agreement’s foundation changes
- Denmark and Greenland could then demand termination of defense agreement and US military withdrawal

**Significance**: Demonstrates Greenlandic population’s strong commitment to self-determination

## SECTION 9: DANISH GOVERNMENT POSITIONS & NATO FRAMEWORK

### 9.1 Danish Constitutional Position

- **Prime Minister Mette Frederiksen** (January 2025): “If the United States chooses to attack another NATO country militarily, then everything stops. That is, including our NATO…”
- **Foreign Minister Lars Løkke Rasmussen**: Denmark categorically rejects any suggestion that Greenland is “not for sale” or subject to external pressure
- **Parliamentary Position**: All political parties united in support of Greenlandic sovereignty

### 9.2 NATO Article 5 - Collective Defense

- Article 5 of NATO treaty: “An armed attack against one or more of them shall be considered an attack against them all”
- **Application**: US military action against Greenland would trigger Article 5; NATO members would be obligated to defend Denmark
- **Paradox**: US cannot simultaneously remain in NATO and unilaterally seize NATO member territory

### 9.3 European Solidarity Statement (January 2025)

- Joint statement from France, Germany, Italy, Poland, Spain, Britain
- **Declaration**: “Greenland belongs to its people. It is for Denmark and Greenland, and them only, to decide on matters concerning Denmark and Greenland.”
- **Significance**: International diplomatic consensus against acquisition

## SECTION 10: CONSTITUTIONAL & LEGAL BARRIERS

### 10.1 Danish Constitutional Law

- Greenland’s status as self-governing territory cannot be unilaterally changed by Denmark
- Transfer of Greenland to third power would require:
1. Amendment to Danish Constitution
1. Danish Parliamentary approval (supermajority)
1. Greenlandic referendum approval
1. Greenlandic Parliament approval
- **Current Status**: All four requirements would be rejected by current majorities

### 10.2 US Constitutional Law

- Acquisition of new territory raises constitutional questions about incorporation, citizenship status
- Congress would need to approve any territorial acquisition
- Indigenous peoples in acquired territory would have rights under US federal Indian law
- **Challenge**: Federal Indian Law doctrine of “trust responsibility” would apply; Kalaallit indigenous rights would be protected

### 10.3 International Law - No Unilateral Territorial Change

- Greenland cannot be transferred without consent of both:
1. Kingdom of Denmark (sovereign authority)
1. Greenlandic people (right to self-determination)
- Unilateral US action would constitute:
  - Violation of UN Charter
  - War crime under international law
  - Breach of NATO treaty obligations
  - Violation of all instruments listed in Section 1 above

## APPENDIX A: KEY DEFINITIONS

**Kalaallit**: Greenlandic Inuit people; officially recognized indigenous people of Greenland

**Kalaallit Nunaat**: “Land of the Kalaallit” - Greenlandic name for Greenland (now preferred term in legal documents)

**Inatsisartut**: Greenlandic Parliament; primary legislative body for Greenland

**Naalakkersuisut**: Greenlandic government (executive branch)

**Self-Determination**: Right of a people to choose their political status and determine their own future under international law

**Sovereignty**: Supreme authority of a state over its territory and people; cannot be diminished by external parties

**Jus Cogens**: Peremptory norm of international law; non-derogable; binding on all states regardless of treaty

**Rebus Sic Stantibus**: Doctrine allowing termination of treaties when fundamental circumstances change

## CONCLUSION

Greenland is protected by:

1. **Explicit international law frameworks** (UN declarations, covenants, ILO conventions)
1. **Formal recognition of indigenous sovereignty** (Self-Government Act 2009)
1. **Bilateral defense agreements** that explicitly protect Danish/Greenlandic sovereignty (1951, 2004)
1. **Democratic mandate** (85% of Greenlanders oppose acquisition)
1. **NATO framework** (mutual defense obligations that preclude US unilateral action)
1. **Circumpolar indigenous solidarity** (ICC support from all Inuit nations)
1. **International legal consensus** (EU, NATO members, UN framework)

Any military action to acquire Greenland would constitute:

- Violation of UN Charter Article 2(4)
- Violation of multiple human rights treaties
- War crime under international law
- Breach of NATO treaty obligations
- Attack on indigenous rights framework affecting all indigenous peoples globally

This consolidation provides legal tools for indigenous advocates, international lawyers, and global indigenous networks to defend Greenlandic sovereignty and, by extension, indigenous rights everywhere.
